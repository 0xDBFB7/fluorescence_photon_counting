Filters

Certainly the most critical aspect of any fluorescence technique is the filter set used.

Important characteristics include transmission % inside the passband, optical density outside the passband, and sharp edges without long tails crossing the so-called Stokes shift(“Stokes Shift” 2021) between absorption and emission. Some filters have ripple far from the edge of interest which must be taken into account when assessing the overall filtering performance.

One might expect, given that white noise has an expectation value of zero, that this technique would average out to 0, and the signal would be linearly proportional to time. However, a 1-dimensional random walk with a uniform step size of 1 is expected to end up \(\sqrt{N}\) units away from the origin after N steps. In the same time, a fluorophore emitting at a rate of \(r=1\) counts per step has an expected value of N counts (a Poisson distribution has a mean equal to its parameter). Therefore, \(\text{SNR} = \frac{N}{\sqrt{N}} = \sqrt{N}\). Longer exposure times would therefore provide better precision, but with diminishing returns.
